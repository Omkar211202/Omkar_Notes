
### Reskilling in the Age of AI  
  
This PDF, "Reskilling in the Age of AI," published in the Harvard Business Review, discusses the critical need for companies to invest in reskilling their workforces in response to the rapid advancements in automation technologies, including generative AI1. The authors, Jorge Tamayo, Leila Doumi, Sagar Goel, Orsolya Kovács-Ondrejkovic, and Raffaella Sadun, highlight that the demand for skills is changing at an accelerated pace, with the average half-life of skills now less than five years, and even lower in some tech fields2.... The OECD predicts that within 15 to 20 years, automation could eliminate 14% of jobs and radically transform another 32%, a figure that doesn't even account for the impact of recent generative AI advancements1.  
  
The authors argue that upskilling alone will not be sufficient, and millions of workers may need to be entirely reskilled to change occupations3.... While many organizations are investing in upskilling, often up to 1.5% of their total budgets4, few have done so effectively at scale6. This leads to the central question of what companies must do to make a reskilling revolution happen6.  
  
Drawing on their research at the HBS Digital Reskilling Lab and the BCG Henderson Institute, including interviews with leaders at nearly 40 organizations with large-scale reskilling programs7, the authors identify five emerging paradigm shifts in reskilling that companies need to understand and embrace to adapt to the era of automation and AI5....  
  
Here's a detailed breakdown of these five paradigm shifts:  
  
1. **Reskilling is a Strategic Imperative**: Traditionally, reskilling was seen as a way to soften layoffs and improve public relations8. However, leading companies now recognize reskilling as a strategic imperative driven by a constrained labor market due to an aging population, new occupations, and the need for company-specific skills8. Effective reskilling allows companies to build a competitive advantage by developing talent not readily available and filling critical skills gaps3.... Examples include Infosys reskilling over 2,000 cybersecurity experts, Vodafone aiming to fill 40% of software developer needs with internal talent, and Amazon's Machine Learning University enabling employees to become ML experts9. Some companies now consider reskilling a core part of their employee value proposition and a strategic tool for balancing workforce supply and demand, with companies like Mahindra & Mahindra, Wipro, Ericsson, and McDonald's providing resources and platforms to promote reskilling9. Additionally, companies like ICICI Bank and CVS are using reskilling to tap into broader talent pools and attract candidates who might not otherwise be considered for open positions10.  
  
•  
  
2. Reskilling is the Responsibility of Every Leader and Manager: Traditionally, reskilling is often siloed within HR11. However, for reskilling initiatives to succeed, they need a profound commitment from HR but also the understanding of the strategic relevance from the rest of the organization to obtain the necessary distributed effort11.... In most interviewed organizations, senior leaders, often CEOs and COOs, visibly champion reskilling and articulate its connection to strategy, ensuring that leadership and management teams understand their shared responsibility12. For example, Ericsson has a multiyear strategy for upskilling and reskilling as part of its digital transformation, defining critical skills and setting targets reviewed by executives quarterly12. Similarly, at CVS, each business leader is responsible for designing and delivering workforce-reskilling plans, factored into performance assessments, and Amazon prominently mentions reskilling in its leadership manifesto13.  
3.  Reskilling is a Change-Management Initiative: Implementing ambitious reskilling programs requires more than just training; it demands creating an organizational context conducive to success, including the right mindset and behaviors among employees and managers14. This makes reskilling akin to a change-management initiative requiring simultaneous focus on several tasks14.  
3. Understanding supply and demand: Companies need a sophisticated understanding of internal and external skills and the skills needed for competitive advantage. A skill taxonomy is a useful tool, and many companies now rely on external providers for this15. However, mapping skills to jobs and resolving disagreements between divisions is crucial15.  
4. Forecasting future skills needs: Leaders must determine future skill requirements based on their strategic objectives, necessitating a rigorous strategic workforce planning methodology, as demonstrated by Allianz translating forecasted business growth into talent demand16.  
5. Recruiting and evaluating reskilled workers: Traditional recruitment based on degrees and experience doesn't work for reskilled workers. A well-developed skill taxonomy can help think in terms of skill adjacencies17. Companies must also develop clear enrollment criteria, considering motivation and personality traits17. Novartis uses an AI-powered internal talent marketplace to match employees to roles based on skills and goals17.  
6. ***Shaping the mindset of middle managers***: Middle managers may resist reskilling due to concerns about workload and talent loss (talent hoarding)18. Addressing this involves making talent development an explicit managerial responsibility, as Wipro and Amazon do by evaluating and promoting managers based on team development18. Involving managers in program design and delivery and providing bias training can also help overcome resistance to hiring reskilled employees18. Senior leaders' role modeling is vital18.  
7. ***Building skills in the flow of work***: Taking employees away for training can be costly and challenging. Adults prefer on-the-job learning19. The best approach involves shadowing, internal apprenticeships, and trial periods, as exemplified by ICICI Bank's vocational residency and field internship19.  
8. ***Matching and integrating reskilled employees***: Clearly describing destination roles increases employee interest and program effectiveness20. Once in new roles, reskilled employees need support with new work norms, culture, networks, and soft skills, where coaching and mentoring are effective. Amazon's buddy system and career coaching programs are examples20.  
9. Employees Want to Reskill—When It Makes Sense: While past data suggests low participation in training programs21, BCG data indicates that 68% of workers are aware of disruptions and willing to reskill22. The key is to treat workers respectfully and clearly communicate the benefits of participation22. Organizations can achieve this by:  
10. Treating employees as partners: Be open about the rationale for reskilling programs and involve employees in their creation. Aligning with worker councils and unions early on is critical23....  
11. Designing programs from the employee point of view: Reduce risk, cost, and effort, and aim for (almost) guaranteed outcomes. Amazon's Career Choice program covering all costs upfront and CVS's "train in place" model are effective examples24.25 highlights the importance of reducing the risk, cost, and effort for participants.  
12. Dedicating adequate time and attention: Reskilling for occupational change requires intensive learning, so employees need sufficient time and mental space26. Vodafone dedicates learning days, and Bosch's Mission to Move program covers tuition and learning time26. For hourly workers, companies like Iberdrola consider training hours as work hours and pay accordingly27.  
13. Reskilling Takes a Village: Companies have traditionally viewed reskilling as an internal challenge28. However, the authors argue that reskilling happens within an ecosystem involving governments, industry, academia, and NGOs28. Coalitions can be more effective than individual organizations28. Companies should harness this wider ecosystem by:  
14. Considering industry partnerships: Instead of competing for talent, companies can collaborate on joint training efforts, share knowledge and resources, and reassure participants of broader opportunities. The Technology in Finance Immersion Programme in Singapore and the Automotive Skills Alliance in the EU are examples29....  
15. Partnering with nonprofits to reach diverse talent: Nonprofits often work with underrepresented populations, allowing companies to expand access to talent cost-effectively. Examples include partnerships with OneTen, Year Up, Joblinge, and RISE 2.031.... Year Up's high placement rate demonstrates the impact of such collaborations32.  
16. Partnering with local colleges and training providers: Collaborating with educational institutions can provide practical technical training and help companies quickly adapt to new technologies. The UK-government-funded Institutes of Technology and BMW's partnership with German agencies are examples32.  
In conclusion, the authors emphasize that while many companies understand the need for these reskilling paradigm shifts and have made commitments, their efforts are often limited by a lack of rigor in measurement and evaluation and insufficient information on how to generalize and scale up successful program features33. To adapt to the accelerating pace of technological change, companies must develop systematic, rigorous, experimental, and long-term approaches to learning from their reskilling investments. Only then will the reskilling revolution truly take of.  
  
  
  
### As the world shifts..  
  
This PDF, titled "As the world shifts so should leaders," argues that **effective leadership is context-specific and depends on the ability to recognize and adapt to the prevailing zeitgeist**. The authors' research of 20th-century American business leaders revealed that their success was less about enduring traits and more about their capacity to understand and capitalize on the opportunities presented by a particular era.  
  
The zeitgeist is shaped by six factors: **global events, government intervention, labor relations, demographics, social mores, and the technology landscape**. Leaders who can identify shifts in these factors and leverage them possess "contextual intelligence".  
  
The article uses the example of **Apple's leadership transition** to illustrate the importance of contextual intelligence. Steve Jobs thrived in the 2000s through breakthrough product innovations, while Tim Cook, in an era of increased competition after Jobs's death in 2011, has focused on services and the iOS ecosystem, adapting to the changing times. Cook's responsiveness to social concerns like LGBTQ issues also demonstrates this adaptability.  
  
The author, Nitin Nohria, former dean of Harvard Business School, argues that we are currently experiencing a **zeitgeist shift** due to the Covid-19 pandemic becoming endemic and the war in Ukraine reprising the Cold War. He then revisits the six factors of the zeitgeist in this new context:  
  
- **Global events:** The war in Ukraine and China and Russia's challenge to American dominance have radically altered the geopolitical situation, forcing leaders to reconsider their global footprints and supply chains. The era of expansive globalization may be ending, with a move towards localization for resilience.  
- **Government intervention:** In the US, political polarization creates legislative uncertainty. However, the significant government response to the pandemic and efforts to curb inflation, along with proposals for tech regulation and wealth taxes, suggest increased government intervention in the future.  
- **Labor relations:** Workers are reevaluating their careers and their relationship with work. Examples include unionization efforts at Amazon, demands for autonomy, and the growth of the gig economy. Simultaneously, AI advancements may displace existing jobs, requiring leaders to rethink the future of work.  
- **Demographics:** Falling fertility rates and a shrinking working-age population in the US, coupled with a large retiring population, will lead to a workforce dominated by Millennials and Gen Z. This presents challenges like rising healthcare costs and potential intergenerational political tensions, as well as opportunities for businesses to segment by age, considering the differing preferences of digital natives and older generations.  
- **Social mores:** Open discussion about all topics, fueled by generational shifts and social media, is now the norm. There's an expectation for leaders and employers to take stances on controversial issues. The rapid prioritization of diversity, equity, and inclusion exemplifies this shift, and other issues like inequality and climate change will also demand attention.  
- **Technology:** While established social media continues to impact society, newer technologies like fintech, crypto, Web3, and the metaverse are emerging. The declining stock prices of many 2021 tech IPOs could lead to consolidation in some sectors, requiring different leadership qualities than periods of creation.  
  
Given this new zeitgeist, the article discusses the **kind of leaders needed now**:  
  
- Executives with the **instincts to navigate shifting external forces**, the **ability to identify new economic opportunities**, and the **skills to lead and manage in this different age**.  
- **Entrepreneurs** who can identify and develop innovations in various areas, including tools supporting remote work, streaming, and telehealth.  
- **Managers** skilled at leveraging economies of scale, scope, and consolidating industries in maturing fields like cloud computing, SaaS, and cybersecurity.  
- Leaders adept at **restructuring and reinvention** in declining sectors like brick-and-mortar retail, branch banking, manufacturing, and distribution.  
- Executives with a **knack for understanding the role of politics and public opinion in decision-making**, as the costs of miscalculations are increasing. The example of Disney's CEO, Bob Chapek, facing criticism for his initial response to Florida's controversial law on gender identity and sexual orientation in schools highlights this.  
  
The article emphasizes the need for leaders to **broaden their thinking about what is relevant to their business** and to empathize with diverse stakeholders. This requires **gathering a wide range of views and listening carefully**. Such leaders are described as "diplomats" or "statespeople," who can not only understand different constituencies but also rally them forward. Examples include Ken Chenault and Ken Frazier's leadership in the corporate response to Black Lives Matter through the OneTen initiative, and Larry Fink's efforts to mobilize investors on long-term sustainability.  
  
The new era also necessitates a **greater emphasis on crisis management skills**. Companies must prepare for a constant stream of upheavals and develop in-house capabilities to anticipate, plan for, and organize around potential challenges, such as the ongoing debates surrounding abortion rights.  
  
Finally, the article notes the increasing importance of other talents for business leaders, including **adroit use of social media, motivating purpose-driven employees, satisfying all stakeholders, and driving digital transformation**.  
  
The central point of the article is that the business environment undergoes significant shifts every decade or two, creating new opportunities and demanding changes in leadership approaches. The current period is clearly one such shift, and **smart leaders will recognize the implications and prepare accordingly**.  
  
  
### Reinventing Globalization  
  
This PDF, titled "13 Reinventing Globalization," argues that **globalization is undergoing a significant transformation, shifting from a focus on efficiency to one prioritizing security and resilience**. The authors contend that after a period of rapid economic integration in the 1990s and 2000s, characterized by "hyper-efficient globalisation," the world is now witnessing a reimagining of global capitalism driven by the pandemic and the war in Ukraine.  
  
The document highlights that the previous era of globalization, where companies located production where costs were lowest and investors deployed capital where returns were highest, led to sophisticated global value chains and lifted many out of poverty. However, this model also created problems, including volatile capital flows, job losses in rich countries, and, more recently, the fragility of lean supply chains and a dependency on autocracies. Disruptions like the Tohoku earthquake in 2011 and the COVID-19 pandemic exposed the vulnerabilities of these interconnected systems.  
  
The **new phase of globalization** is characterized by several key shifts:  
  
- **Prioritization of Security over Efficiency:** The primary driver is no longer solely cost reduction but also the reliability and security of supply chains, leading firms to prioritize doing business with trusted partners in friendly countries.  
- **Increased Inventories:** Companies are building up precautionary inventories as insurance against shortages and inflation. The largest 3,000 firms globally have increased their inventories from 6% to 9% of world GDP since 2016.  
- **Dual Sourcing and Longer-Term Contracts:** Firms are adopting strategies to diversify their supplier base and secure longer-term agreements.  
- **Shift in Multinational Investment:** A larger proportion of multinational investment now comes from local subsidiaries reinvesting locally, rather than parent firms sending capital across borders, echoing trends from the 1930s.  
- **Vertical Integration:** Industries, like the car industry (following Tesla's model), are moving towards greater vertical integration to control their supply chains from raw materials to component design. The American computer sector has seen a significant increase in vertical integration.  
- **Diversification Away from China:** While official FDI figures into China may seem strong, other measures like "greenfield" FDI (capital for new facilities) show a decline in China's share since the mid-2000s. OECD countries' greenfield FDI into China has decreased, and many American multinationals are increasing recruitment in other parts of Asia like the Philippines and India. Imports of intermediate goods from Vietnam by OECD countries have grown significantly more than those from China.  
- **Regionalization of Supply Chains:** There's a growing trend towards regionalizing supply chains, with increased investment in countries like Mexico to serve North American demand. Trade deals like the US-Mexico-Canada Agreement can facilitate this restructuring.  
- **Industrial Policy:** Governments worldwide are increasingly implementing industrial policies to promote self-reliance and international pre-eminence in strategic technologies. Over 90% of the world's GDP is now covered by countries with formal industrial strategies. This includes subsidies and export restrictions.  
- **Focus on Resilience:** Decision-makers are more concerned with the robustness of supply chains than just their efficiency.  
  
The document also notes some potential **challenges and uncertainties** associated with this shift:  
  
- **Risk of Protectionism:** The pursuit of security could lead to excessive protectionism, big government intervention, and worsening inflation.  
- **Increased Costs:** Taking security into account will likely increase costs for taxpayers, companies, and consumers. Duplicating a quarter of all multinational activity could add significant operating and financial costs.  
- **Potential for Volatility and Fragmentation:** Short-term effects of the shift could include more volatility and fragmentation in the global economy.  
- **Limited Reshoring:** Despite the talk, there is little evidence of significant reshoring of production to rich countries. Imports continue to form a large share of domestic manufacturing output in America.  
- **The Importance of Diversification:** True resilience comes from diversification of suppliers, not just concentrating production at home. Autocracies control a relatively small portion of critical global trade.  
  
Finally, the article concludes by emphasizing that the structure of global supply chains is clearly changing, moving away from the Sino-centric, market-based system towards a more costly but potentially less fragile global arrangement. This transformation is driven by a concern for economic dependency and a desire for greater security in a world facing increasing geopolitical tensions and climate change. The long-term consequences of this "reinvented globalization" remain to be seen, but it represents a significant departure from the trends of the past few decades.  
  
### India's ESG readiness  
  
This PDF, titled "09 How ESG Ready is india.pdf," discusses the **growing significance of Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) factors for businesses in India** and examines the readiness of Indian companies in adopting and reporting ESG practices.  
  
The PDF highlights that **ESG is now integral to business continuity** and is considered **material and important in an economic sense**, moving beyond just a profound philosophical leap. The realization that ESG parameters need to be built into the business scorecard across functions with due weightage, supported by thorough materiality assessment, is gaining momentum.  
  
Several factors are driving the increasing focus on ESG in India:  
  
- **Investor Interest:** Investors are increasingly considering ESG impacts and risks when making investment decisions. Financial institutions are playing a pivotal role in catalyzing sustainable development by integrating ESG into their investment strategies.  
- **Regulatory Push:** Mandatory disclosure requirements from bodies like the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) for listed companies have increased the focus on ESG reporting. SEBI's mandate for the top 1,000 listed companies to prepare Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reports (BRSR) is a key step. Earlier mandates included the Business Responsibility Report (BRR).  
- **Stakeholder Expectations:** Consumers, particularly the younger generation, are increasingly conscious of environmental and social issues and expect companies to operate sustainably. Society demands that businesses define the purpose of business beyond profit and consider their impact on habitats and future generations.  
- **Global Trends:** The increasing global emphasis on sustainability and climate change, along with international reporting frameworks like the International Integrated Reporting Council and the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD), are influencing the Indian business landscape.  
- **Business Strategy:** Integrating ESG is seen as crucial for long-term business success and resilience. It can lead to better risk management, resource efficiency, and enhanced reputation.  
  
The PDF identifies several **challenges and hurdles** in ESG adoption and reporting in India:  
  
- **Lack of Uniform Standards and Metrics:** A common governance instrument that makes ESG comparability difficult is a challenge. Different geographies follow varying environmental standards. The need for an integrated financial and non-financial reporting framework is emphasized. While the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) is working to standardize sustainability reporting globally, its integration in India needs time.  
- **Data Gaps and Quality:** Gathering accurate, reliable, and comprehensive ESG data is a significant challenge for many companies. This includes data across value chains, especially for companies with complex and geographically spread-out operations.  
- **Complexity of Frameworks:** Navigating the evolving landscape of ESG standards and frameworks (like GRI, SASB, BRSR, TCFD, CDP) can be complex for companies.  
- **Awareness and Understanding:** There's a varying level of awareness and understanding of ESG principles and their strategic importance across different companies, particularly among SMEs.  
- **Greenwashing Concerns:** As ESG becomes more mainstream, the risk of greenwashing (misrepresenting sustainability efforts) increases, necessitating more transparent and credible reporting.  
- **Adoption of ESG Practices:** Beyond reporting, the actual implementation and integration of ESG considerations into core business practices remain a challenge for many.  
  
The PDF highlights the **growing role of technology and data management platforms** in addressing these challenges. Data management platforms are helping businesses make informed decisions related to their environmental, social, and governance performance by collecting and standardizing data. These platforms assist in streamlining ESG reporting, improving data accuracy, and providing insights for better ESG management.  
  
The PDF also mentions the **regulatory landscape and reporting frameworks** in India:  
  
- **SEBI's BRSR Mandate:** This mandate requires detailed disclosures on a wide range of ESG parameters for the top 1,000 listed companies. The BRSR framework aims to bring in greater transparency and accountability in ESG reporting.  
- **Earlier BRR Framework:** This was a precursor to the BRSR and had a less comprehensive scope.  
- **Other Reporting Standards:** While Indian regulations are evolving, many organizations also refer to global standards like GRI, SASB, TCFD, and CDP for their reporting needs.  
  
The **benefits of embracing ESG** highlighted in the PDF include:  
  
- **Enhanced Stakeholder Trust:** Transparent ESG practices can build trust with investors, customers, employees, and the wider community.  
- **Improved Financial Performance:** Integrating ESG can lead to better risk management, operational efficiencies, and potentially lower cost of capital.  
- **Attracting and Retaining Talent:** Companies with strong ESG credentials are often more attractive to purpose-driven employees.  
- **Long-Term Sustainability:** Embedding ESG principles contributes to the long-term viability and resilience of the business.  
- **Access to Green Finance:** As the focus on sustainable finance grows, companies with strong ESG performance may have better access to green bonds and other sustainable financing options.  
  
The PDF concludes by emphasizing that **ESG is no longer a niche area but a fundamental aspect of doing business responsibly and sustainably in India**. While challenges remain in terms of standardization, data quality, and implementation, the increasing regulatory push, investor interest, and stakeholder awareness are driving Indian companies towards greater ESG readiness. The adoption of technology and data management solutions is crucial in this journey.
